How Octagon AI works

What the model actually does, what the numbers mean, and where it falls short. No statistics degree required.

The big idea
A model trained on 8,337 UFC fights

Octagon AI is a machine learning model that studied every UFC fight since 1994 — striking stats, takedown numbers, records, physical attributes, fight outcomes. It learned which patterns tend to predict winners, then we tested it on fights it had never seen before.

It doesn't watch video or read fight news. It reads numbers — how accurately a fighter lands strikes, how often they get taken down, their recent form, and how their skill rating compares to their opponent. Think of it as a very well-informed second opinion before you decide who to bet on.

How it learned
What it looks at for every fight

For each fight, the model computes the difference between both fighters across 32 categories. It's not asking "is Fighter A good?" — it's asking "is Fighter A better than Fighter B in the areas that matter for this specific matchup?"

1
Striking
Significant strike accuracy, strikes landed per minute, strikes absorbed per minute, strike defense percentage. Who lands cleaner and who eats more damage.
2
Grappling
Takedown accuracy, takedown defense, submission attempts, control time on the ground. Who dominates when the fight hits the mat.
3
Recent form
Win streaks, performance in the last 3 and 5 fights, days since last fight. Fresh fighters on winning runs tend to outperform their career averages.
4
Elo skill rating
A career rating that adjusts based on who you beat. Knocking out a top contender moves your score up more than beating a newcomer. Everyone starts at 1,500 — Jon Jones sits at 1,849.
5
Physical attributes
Reach difference, height difference, age. These matter less than most people think but are included for completeness.
Reading the picks
What the confidence levels mean

Every prediction comes with a confidence label. This reflects how much the model's signals agree with each other — not just the win probability number. A fight can have a 70% probability but still be flagged low confidence if the data is noisy or contradictory.

✅
High confidence
The model gives the predicted winner 75% or higher, and the signals all point the same direction. Historically right about 9 out of 10 times in testing. These are the picks worth paying attention to.
⚡
Medium confidence
The model has a clear lean at 60–74% but the fight is competitive. There's an edge here but not a lock. Good for smaller bets or as part of a parlay — not something to go heavy on.
🎲
Low confidence
Below 60% — essentially a coin flip. The model has a very weak lean or the data is contradictory. Treat these as entertainment, not betting advice. Any positive EV on these should be viewed skeptically.
🚫
Avoid — conflicting signals
The statistical model and the Elo rating system are pointing at different winners. Maximum uncertainty. Even if there's positive EV showing, the model is essentially guessing. Skip these.
Unpredictability
The chaos score

Each fight gets a chaos score between 0 and 1. It measures how unpredictable a fighter's history is — based on how randomly they win by KO versus submission versus decision, and whether fights tend to end early or go the distance.

Under 0.45 means the fight is relatively predictable — good for high confidence picks. Over 0.65 means expect the unexpected. Two high-chaos fighters in one fight is a coin flip regardless of what the model says.

The odds
What EV means in plain English

EV stands for Expected Value. It answers one question: based on what the model thinks the fighter's true win probability is, are the betting odds offering good value or bad value?

When the model thinks a fighter has a 70% chance to win but the odds imply only a 50% chance, that's a gap worth noting. When the model and the odds agree, EV is close to zero.

Example — Fighter A at +200 (underdog)
What the odds imply their chance is 33%
What the model thinks their chance is 52%
Gap between model and odds +19%
EV per $100 bet +$42

Positive EV means the model sees more value in the bet than the odds reflect. It does not mean the fighter will win — it means if this situation repeated itself many times, you'd come out ahead on average. Any single fight can still go wrong.

⚠️
EV is a long-run concept. MMA is chaotic — upsets happen constantly. Never use EV as a reason to bet more than you're comfortable losing. Use it as one input in your decision, alongside the fighter breakdown and your own knowledge of the sport. This is not financial advice.
Quick reference
What each number on the card means
ML
Moneyline odds (e.g. +153 or -184)
American betting odds. Positive (+153) = underdog — bet $100 to win $153 profit. Negative (-184) = favorite — bet $184 to win $100 profit. The bigger the negative number, the more heavily favored that fighter is.
%
Octagon AI probability
What the model thinks each fighter's chance of winning is. This is based purely on historical stats — not tape, not fight news, not gut feel. Use it alongside the fighter breakdown to form your own view.
EV
Expected Value (e.g. +$42)
The average profit per $100 bet if the model's probability is correct over many repetitions. Positive = the odds offer value relative to the model. Negative = the odds don't justify the bet at face value. Always check confidence level before acting on EV.
ELO
Elo rating (e.g. 1625)
A career skill score. Everyone starts at 1,500. Beating higher-rated opponents moves your score up more. A gap of 100+ points is significant. Under 50 points difference is essentially a toss-up on ratings alone.
CH
Chaos score (e.g. 0.42)
How unpredictable a fighter's history is. Under 0.45 = more predictable. Over 0.65 = expect the unexpected. Two chaotic fighters in one fight means the confidence label matters more than the probability number.
⚡
Elo clash
The statistical model and the Elo rating system are pointing at different winners. This flag means increased upset risk — the model is less certain than the probability number suggests. Treat these picks with extra caution.
Honesty
Where it falls short

No model predicts MMA perfectly. Here's where ours struggles most — and what you should do about it.

1
One-punch knockout artists
Fighters like Alex Pereira or Francis Ngannou can end any fight with a single shot regardless of stats. Raw power doesn't show up cleanly in numbers. These fights get flagged low confidence — trust that flag.
2
New and debut fighters
Fewer than 5 UFC fights means not enough data to build a reliable profile. The model defaults to Elo-only predictions for these fighters and flags them clearly on the card.
3
Fight week news
Injuries, bad weight cuts, camp drama, last-minute opponent changes — none of this is in historical stats. Always check fight news before acting on any prediction. The model has no idea what happened this week.
4
Women's divisions
Less historical data than the men's divisions means less reliable predictions. Take women's division picks with an extra grain of salt, especially for fighters with shorter UFC track records.
