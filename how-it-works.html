How Octagon AI works

No statistics degree required. Here's exactly how we predict UFC fights — and what our numbers actually mean.

The big idea
A computer that studied 8,337 fights

Octagon AI is a machine learning model like a very attentive student who watched every UFC fight since 1994, took detailed notes on striking, takedowns, knockdowns, records, and physical attributes, then got tested on fights it had never seen before. It correctly predicted the winner 83% of the time on those unseen fights.

It doesn't watch video. It reads numbers, for example, how accurately a fighter lands strikes, how often they get taken down, their recent form, and how their Elo rating compares to their opponent's.

How it learned
Training, testing, and making sure it's not cheating

The model was trained only on fights from 1994–2021. It was then tested on fights from 2022–2025 — fights it had never seen and couldn't have memorized.

1
Collect the data
Every UFC fight since 1994 — striking stats, grappling stats, physical measurements, fight outcomes, and more. Over 124 data points per fight.
2
Build fighter profiles
For each fight, we compute the difference between both fighters across 32 categories — striking accuracy, takedown defense, recent form, and so on. The model looks at gaps, not just raw numbers.
3
Train the model
A machine learning algorithm called XGBoost finds patterns across thousands of fights — things like "fighters with better striking accuracy and a recent win streak win more often." It runs 150 rounds of self-improvement to find the best version of itself.
4
Test it honestly
The model is evaluated only on fights after 2023 — ones it never trained on. If it does well there, the accuracy is real. Our model scored 83.1% on this blind test.
Fighter ratings
Elo — the same system used in chess

Every fighter starts with an Elo rating of 1,500 — like a baseline score. When you beat a higher-rated opponent, your score goes up a lot. Beat a weaker opponent and it barely moves. Lose to a heavy underdog and your score drops sharply.

This gives every fighter a single number that captures their career trajectory. Jon Jones is at 1,849. A debut fighter sits at 1,500 until they prove themselves. The model uses this Elo difference as one of its inputs — but it's not the only thing it looks at.

Reading the predictions
What the confidence levels mean

Every prediction comes with a confidence badge. This isn't just about the probability number — it also factors in how "chaotic" a fight is likely to be and whether the model's different signals agree with each other.

✅
Safe pick — model 75%+
The model gives the predicted winner a 75% or higher chance of winning. Example: Islam Makhachev vs a late-career opponent — model at 88%, consistent grappling stats, clear edge. This is the band worth acting on.
⚡
Strong lean — ranking mismatch
Model is 75%+ confident in the predicted winner, but the Elo ranking system favors the other fighter historically. This means the public and oddsmakers may be backing the wrong fighter — creating a potential value opportunity. Example: Zhang Mingyang at 92.6% but Elo favors Menifield — if Vegas prices Zhang as an underdog, you're getting good odds on a near-certain model favorite. This is the chip to look for when hunting value bets.
⚠️
Moderate pick — model 60–74%
The model has a clear lean but the fight is competitive. Example: Pavlovich at 65% over Teixeira — statistical edge exists but the margin is close enough to stay cautious. Reasonable lean, not a lock.
🎲
Coin flip — model below 60%
The model gives the predicted winner less than 60% — essentially a 50/50. Example: Perez vs Sumudaerji at 53.6%. Both fighters are unpredictable and the model has very low conviction. Treat skeptically regardless of EV.
🚫
Avoid — conflicting signals
Below 60% win probability AND Elo clash detected — the model and ranking system both disagree on who wins. Maximum uncertainty. Example: a chaotic striker vs a historically ranked grappler where neither signal is reliable.
The odds
What is EV and why does it matter?

EV stands for "Expected Value." It answers a simple question: if you bet $100 on this fighter over and over again, would you make money or lose money in the long run?

Vegas sets odds based on where people are betting — not necessarily who they think will win. Sometimes the crowd is wrong, and our model spots a gap between what Vegas implies and what the data says. That gap is called "edge."

Example — Fighter A at +200 (underdog)
Vegas implied win probability 33%
Our model's win probability 52%
Edge (model minus Vegas) +19%
EV per $100 bet +$42

A positive EV means the bet is mathematically underpriced by Vegas. It does not mean the fighter will definitely win — it means the odds are in your favor if you made this same bet many times.

⚠️
Important: Positive EV is a long-run concept. Any single fight can go either way — upsets happen constantly in MMA. Never bet more than you're comfortable losing, and treat these as tools for thinking, not guarantees. This is not financial advice.
Honesty
What the model can't do

No model predicts MMA perfectly — and anyone who claims otherwise is lying to you. Here's what ours genuinely struggles with:

1
One-punch knockouts
Fighters like Francis Ngannou or Alex Pereira can end any fight with a single shot. Data can't fully capture raw knockout power. The model knows this and flags those fights as low confidence.
2
New fighters
Fighters with fewer than 5 UFC fights don't have enough data. The model defaults to neutral assumptions, which makes those picks less reliable.
3
Things that happen outside the cage
Injuries, weight cut issues, personal problems, last-minute opponent changes — none of this shows up in historical stats. Always read the fight news before relying on any prediction.
Quick reference
How to read the numbers

Every fight card shows a handful of metrics. Here's exactly what each one means in plain English.

SP
Safe pick / Strong lean
Model gives the winner 75%+. Safe pick means all signals agree. Strong lean means the model is confident but Elo disagrees — still a good pick, just watch for upsets. The signal bars show fight chaos independently.
MP
Moderate pick / Risky
Model gives the winner 60–74%. Moderate pick means a reasonable lean. Risky means 60–74% but Elo also disagrees — consider sizing down or skipping entirely.
CF
Coin flip / Avoid
Model gives the winner below 60%. Coin flip means near-even odds. Avoid means below 60% AND Elo clash — maximum uncertainty, both signals are unreliable. Positive EV on these picks should be treated skeptically.
CH
Chaos score (e.g. chaos 0.42)
A number from 0 to 1 measuring how unpredictable each fighter's history is — based on how randomly they win by KO vs submission vs decision, and how often they go early vs late rounds. Lower is more predictable. Under 0.45 = high confidence zone. Over 0.65 = coin flip territory. Two chaos monsters in one fight = expect the unexpected.
⚡
Elo clash detected
The model's statistical analysis and the Elo ranking system are pointing in opposite directions. For example: Elo says Fighter A is clearly better historically, but the stats say Fighter B should win this specific matchup. When these signals conflict, upset risk goes up. This flag correctly identified the Strickland and Oliveira upsets before they happened.
EV
+$42 EV (Expected Value)
If you bet $100 on this fighter repeatedly in identical situations, you'd make $42 profit on average per bet. Positive EV means Vegas is underpricing the fighter relative to what the model thinks their true win probability is. This is a long-run concept — any single fight can still go wrong.
ML
ML +153 / ML -184 (Moneyline odds)
American betting odds. Positive (+153) means underdog — bet $100 to win $153 profit. Negative (-184) means favorite — bet $184 to win $100 profit. The bigger the negative number, the more heavily favored Vegas thinks that fighter is.
ELO
Elo 1625 (Fighter rating)
A career skill rating. Everyone starts at 1500. Beating a higher-rated opponent moves your score up more than beating a weaker one. Jon Jones sits at 1849 — the highest in UFC history in this dataset. A gap of 100+ Elo points is significant. Under 50 points is essentially a toss-up on ratings alone.
